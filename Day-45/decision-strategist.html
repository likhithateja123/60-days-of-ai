1Decision summary

The decision: Whether to prioritize placement/software job preparation or spend more time building AI projects and skills, in order to secure a software or AI-related role after graduation while building toward a long-term AI engineering career.

Main trade-off: Time invested in AI depth versus time invested in placement readiness — both compete for the same limited hours over the next 6 months.

Why it's hard: Both paths feel urgent and irreversible in the moment. Placements have a hard external deadline; AI mastery is a slow compounding process. Choosing either one too hard risks losing ground on the other, and the fear of missing out cuts both ways.

2Compare options

Option A — Placement-first

Advantage
  • Faster path to income and job security
  • Structured, well-known prep process (DSA, aptitude, interviews)
  • Reduces anxiety about missing campus opportunities
Disadvantage
  • Less time to build a distinctive AI portfolio
  • Risk of landing a generic software role misaligned with long-term goals
Hidden opportunity

Strong DSA/CS fundamentals actually make you a better AI engineer later — this isn't wasted time, it's infrastructure.

Best for: Reducing financial/career risk while keeping AI as a strong side-track.

Option B — AI-project-first

Advantage
  • Builds a differentiated portfolio for AI-specific roles
  • Compounds toward your actual long-term goal faster
  • Projects can double as interview talking points
Disadvantage
  • Risk of under-preparing for placement interviews (DSA, CS fundamentals)
  • AI projects can sprawl without a job-relevant deadline forcing focus
Hidden opportunity

A few sharp, well-documented AI projects can itself become your placement differentiator, not a distraction from it.

Best for: Long-term career alignment if you can protect a placement-prep baseline.

3Assumption check

3 assumptions you're making

  • That placement prep and AI project work are mutually exclusive rather than sequenced or blended.
  • That missing a campus placement cycle closes the door on software jobs entirely.
  • That "enough" AI skill is a fixed bar you must hit before the 6-month deadline, rather than an ongoing trajectory.

2 thinking biases at play

  • False dichotomy: Framing this as "placements OR AI" when a blended, sequenced approach is very possible in 6 months.

1 overlooked factor

Off-campus and pool-campus hiring windows exist well beyond the standard placement season — the "one shot" framing of campus placements is often less rigid than it feels.

5Risk preview

AI-project-first — how it could fail

Why it could fail: Projects drift without deadlines, leaving weak DSA/interview readiness when placement season arrives.

Warning signs: Skipping timed practice for weeks, no completed project in 3–4 weeks, avoiding mock interviews.

Prevention: Timebox placement prep daily (non-negotiable), cap each AI project at 2–3 weeks with a shipped output.

Placement-first — how it could fail

Why it could fail: You land a generic role, then long hours make it hard to start the AI journey you actually wanted.

Warning signs: Zero AI learning time per week, choosing prep topics with no AI relevance, feeling resentful about the job search.

Prevention: Protect even 5–6 hrs/week for AI fundamentals; prioritize companies/roles with AI-adjacent teams when applying.

7Final recommendation

Blended path, weighted toward AI projects

Run a dual-track 6 months: protect a daily floor of placement prep (DSA/aptitude, ~1 hr/day) while making AI projects your primary growth engine (~2x the time). This matches your gut instinct while directly hedging your biggest fear on both sides.

One honest takeaway: You don't have to pick a side forever — you have to pick a ratio for the next 6 months, and revisit it monthly.
